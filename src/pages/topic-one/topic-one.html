61kg of plastic

Ireland generates an average of 61kg of plastic waste per person every year, the equivalent of nearly 2,000 water bottles per person annually. Engaging in ways to reduce our plastic consumption is a key environmental necessity. However, before we start, we need to understand why plastic pollution is such a pressing issue, its causes, effects, and viable solutions to this issue.

Plastic

never fully biodegrade

Plastics photodegrade, which means that they break down into smaller, and smaller pieces until they are at a microscopic level. Any toxic additives contained within the plastics are released into the ocean.

97% of the Earth’s water supply is contained in the ocean. Plastic waste is greatly endangering the world’s water supply. Plastics dumped in landfills form dangerous chemicals, and when these chemicals seep underground, they degrade the water quality.

About plastic

Cause

Plastics are cheap and easy to manufacture

Plastics have been used to make countless utilities, including plastic water bottles, straws, food containers, lids, and packaging wrappers.

Plastics are easily and haphazardly discarded

The use of plastic items such as bags, wrappers, bottles, straws and food containers are very typically very short. Whenever plastics are thrown away, they continue to persist within the environment.

Plastic decomposes slowly

Plastics are slow to decompose due to their strong chemical bonds that make plastic items durable. The EPA has stated that “every bit of plastic ever made still exists”. A plastic bottle can take up to 400 years to decompose, while plastic fishing lines can take more than 600 years.

The shipping and fishing industry

are major contributors of plastic pollution. Plastic waste from ships, sea accidents, and nets used for fishing emit toxins into the water which severely affects marine creatures.

About plastic

Effects

Threatens the life of numerous sea creatures

These marine creatures ingest plastic mainly by mistaking it as food, and can also be endangered by plastics through becoming entangled in plastic products such as fishing nets, and can holders. 44% of all seabird species, along with sea turtles, and cetaceans have been documented to have plastic debris in or around their bodies.

Affects humans

Plastic pollution in our oceans severely affects humans in numerous ways. Studies have shown that toxins in plastics can cause several health issues such as cancer, and immune system problems. Ingestion of plastic by fish and other marine creatures finds its way into peoples’ bodies when they consume marine food.

Threatens our ecosystem

Plastic pollution poses a serious threat to our ecosystems. Plastics in our oceans can lead to the invasion of non-indigenous species, and organisms in marine colonies.

Garbage patches

Plastic pollution has resulted in the creation of several floating garbage patches in our oceans called gyres. The Great Pacific Garbage patch located in the North Pacific Ocean off the coast of California is reported to be the largest ocean garbage patch in the world. According to some studies this patch is twice the size of Texas.

About plastic

Solution

Reduce use of single-use plastics

As 60-90% of marine litter is plastic-based, refusing any single-use plastics, reducing our dependence on plastics, and carrying reusable versions of these products will greatly help to reduce the amount of new plastics in circulation.

Recycle

Recycling helps keep single-use plastics out of the ocean, and reduces the amount of new plastics in circulation.

Avoid using products containing microbeads

Microbeads have become a growing source of plastic pollution in recent years. You can avoid products containing microbeads by looking for polythelene and polypropylene on the ingredients label of cosmetic products, and toiletries such as face wash, and shower gel.

Support organisations adressing plastic pollution

Non-profit organisations dealing with the problem of ocean plastic include the Oceanic Society Plastic Soup Foundation, and Plastic Pollution Coalition.
